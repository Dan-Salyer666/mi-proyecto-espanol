Patrones de Uso Verbal

✦
Structural blueprints for decoding Spanish syntax

Categorizing syntax into structural blueprints moves you away from translating word-for-word and trains you to instinctively feel how the language fits together. Select a pattern below to explore its mechanics.

Aviso · The "-le" Suffix Warning Label Attaching "-le" to an infinitive (e.g., darle, gustarle, caerle) is not a grammatical structure itself. It is a shorthand warning label used in grammar guides indicating the verb requires an Indirect Object Pronoun (receiver) to function properly in context. It does not dictate the structural pattern or subject placement.
①
The Standard Action
(Subject) + [IOP] + [DOP] + Verb + (Direct Object)

A subject performs an action, transferring it directly to an object or a receiver. This is your standard SVO active framework.
②
The Reactive Pattern
(A + Pronoun) + IOP + Verb (3rd Person) + Subject

The Gustar logic. An external object or concept functions as the subject, causing a reaction or feeling within a receiver.
③
The Impression Pattern
(Subject) + IOP + Intrans. Verb + Adverb/Adjective

The Caer bien logic. A subject projects a vibe or an outcome that is subsequently judged or experienced by a receiver.
④
The Pronominal Pattern
(Subject) + Reflexive Pronoun + Verb

The action reflects directly back onto the subject, occurs mutually between subjects, or alters the verb's core definition entirely.
⑤
The Copular Pattern
(Subject) + Ser/Estar/Parecer + Adjective/Noun

The verb acts purely as an equals sign, linking a subject to its location, temporary condition, or permanent description.
⑥
The Impersonal "Se"
Se + Verb (3rd person) + (Noun)

Removes the specific actor to establish general facts, societal rules, or universal habits where the driver is irrelevant.
⑦
The Compound Pattern
Helper Verb (Conjugated) + [Prep] + Infinitive/Gerund

Periphrastic structures where two verbs fuse to form a single idea; the first sets tense, the second delivers the action.
