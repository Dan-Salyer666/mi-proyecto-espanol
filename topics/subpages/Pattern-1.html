Patrón 1

The Standard Action

❧
The foundation of active subject-verb-object mechanics

This is your standard active sentence. A subject actively performs a verb, often acting upon a direct object, and sometimes transferring that action to a receiver (an indirect object).

①
Core Mechanicsactive transfer
SUBJECT DRIVES THE ACTION
(Subject) + [IOP] + [DOP] + Verb + (Direct Object)

The verb always conjugates to match the person doing the action. Because the verb ending already indicates who is acting, the subject pronoun is frequently omitted in natural speech.

  • (Nosotros) leemos el libro. We read the book. — Subject acts upon a direct object.
  • Ella me da las llaves. She gives me the keys. — Subject transfers an object to a receiver.
②
The Sympathetic Dativebody parts
PHYSICAL REACTIONS
Definite Article (el/la) + IOP + Intransitive Verb

Spanish generally avoids using possessive adjectives (mi, tu, su) with body parts. Instead, the body part acts as the subject performing the action, and you act as the Indirect Object Pronoun (IOP) receiving the effect.

  • Me tiemblan las piernas. My legs are trembling. — Literally: The legs tremble to me. The legs are the subject driving the plural verb.
  • Le late el corazón. His heart is beating. — Literally: The heart beats to him.
③
The "Darle" Case Studythe word order illusion
DECODING THE DISGUISE

Because Spanish syntax is flexible, receivers (IOPs) are often placed at the front of the sentence. This creates a visual illusion for English speakers, making Pattern 1 look exactly like the Gustar pattern. Never trust word order. Find the verb conjugation first.

  • Me da vueltas la cabeza. My head is spinning. — Physical: The head is the subject giving spins to you. Confusing because "Me" sits at the front.
  • Le doy vueltas a la cosa. I am overthinking the thing. — Mental: "Yo" is the subject giving spins to the concept. The verb "doy" proves you are the driver.
Nota · Uso Rioplatense When applying Pattern 1 in the Rioplatense dialect (Argentina/Uruguay), standard subject pronouns and conjugations shift to voseo in the second person informal.

Instead of "Tú le das vueltas" (You overthink it), the active driver shifts to "Vos le dás vueltas", dropping the 'i' from the peninsular vosotros conjugation and maintaining the accent on the final syllable.
