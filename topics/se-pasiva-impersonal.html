Se Pasiva vs. Se Impersonal

✦
Passive Se vs. Impersonal Se
Spanish uses se in two closely related constructions that look similar but follow different rules. The distinction lives in the verb's agreement — whether it stays singular or matches a plural noun — and in what else appears in the sentence. Three patterns are worth knowing.
①
Se Pasiva
Passive Se
Se + verbo transitivo + cosa(s)

The "thing" acted on is the grammatical subject. If the thing is plural, the verb is plural. This is the most common way Spanish expresses the passive voice — much more natural than ser + participio in everyday language.

  • Se vende un mueble viejo. An old piece of furniture is sold.
  • Se alquilan apartamentos en el centro. Apartments are rented downtown. — plural verb, because "apartamentos" is plural.
  • Se construyó un puente nuevo. A new bridge was built.
②
Se Impersonalcon preposición
Impersonal Se — with preposition
Se + verbo + preposición + …

With a preposition (de, en, con, sobre…), the noun that follows cannot be the grammatical subject. The verb stays strictly singular — always, regardless of whether the following noun is plural.

  • Se habla de un mueble viejo. There is talk about an old piece of furniture.
  • Se confía en el sistema. One trusts in the system.
  • Se vive bien en España. People live well in Spain.
③
Se Impersonalcon "a" personal
Impersonal Se — with personal "a"
Se + verbo + a + persona(s)

When the object of the verb is a specific person or group of people, Spanish inserts the personal a. This blocks the person from becoming the subject, so the verb stays singular — even with plural people. Without the personal "a," the sentence could be misread as reflexive ("teachers respect themselves") or reciprocal.

  • Se busca a los ladrones. The thieves are being looked for.
  • Se entrevistó a los candidatos. The candidates were interviewed. — singular verb; personal "a" blocks agreement.
  • Se respeta mucho a los maestros aquí. Teachers are highly respected here.

¿Cuál uso?

Which pattern to pick — a quick test
The verb is transitive and its object is a thing (no preposition, no personal "a")
→
Se Pasiva verb agrees with the thing
A preposition sits between the verb and what follows
→
Se Impersonal (prep.) verb always singular
The object is a specific person or people, marked by personal "a"
→
Se Impersonal ("a") verb always singular
Nota · Common Ambiguity In conversational speech you may hear the "impersonal" pattern applied even where grammar prescribes plural agreement — for example, "Se vende apartamentos" instead of the prescriptive "Se venden apartamentos." Both are heard; the plural-agreement version (Se Pasiva) remains the standard in writing and careful speech. When in doubt, agree the verb with the thing and you'll be correct.
