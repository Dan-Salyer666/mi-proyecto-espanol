El Mundo del Infinitivo

Un viaje a través de los usos del infinitivo después de las preposiciones

Why Spanish uses the infinitive where English uses -ing, and the six forms that open up once you understand the rule.

The Golden Rule

In Spanish, any verb that immediately follows a preposition must be in the infinitive — the unconjugated form ending in -ar, -er, or -ir. No exceptions. This applies to simple prepositions, compound prepositions, and the special construction "al."

⚠️ The English Trap

English does the opposite. When a verb follows a preposition in English, we use the gerund — the "-ing" form. Your brain will want to map that -ing to Spanish -ando/-iendo. Resist it.

✘ Gracias por viniendo.

✔ Gracias por venir.

Every time you see a preposition + verb in English ("for doing," "without knowing," "before leaving"), train yourself to reach for the infinitive in Spanish, not the gerund.

Las Preposiciones Clave

Preposiciones simples

These are the everyday prepositions that trigger the infinitive rule:

por para de a con en sin hasta tras

Preposiciones compuestas

Multi-word prepositions that end in "de" — the infinitive follows the "de":

antes de después de en vez de a pesar de además de acerca de en lugar de
  • Salimos después de comer. We went out after eating.
  • Lo hizo a pesar de estar cansado. He did it despite being tired.
  • En vez de quejarte, hacé algo. Instead of complaining, do something.

La construcción "al + infinitivo"

This is one of the most common infinitive structures in Spanish. Al (a + el) + infinitive means "upon doing," "when doing," or "on doing" — it marks the moment an action happens.

al + infinitivo = "upon / when [doing]"
  • Al llegar a casa, me di cuenta de que no tenía las llaves. Upon arriving home, I realized I didn't have the keys.
  • Al ver los resultados, se puso a llorar. When she saw the results, she started crying.
  • Al abrir la puerta, encontré el paquete. On opening the door, I found the package.

Usage note: "Al + infinitivo" is extremely common at all levels of speech and writing. You'll hear it constantly in everyday conversation. It's one of the best tools for sounding natural in Spanish.

Los Pronombres con el Infinitivo

Object pronouns (me, te, lo, la, le, nos, los, las, les) and reflexive pronouns (me, te, se, nos) attach to the end of the infinitive. In compound forms, they attach to the first verb in the chain (usually haber).

  • Gracias por ayudarme. Thanks for helping me.
  • Salió sin decirle nada. He left without saying anything to her.
  • Antes de sentarse, miró la silla. Before sitting down, he looked at the chair.
  • Me alegro por haberlo hecho. I'm glad for having done it.
  • Se fue sin haberse despedido. He left without having said goodbye.

Las 6 Formas del Infinitivo

The golden rule says the first verb after the preposition must be an infinitive. But that first verb can be a helping verb (haber, estar, ser), which then opens the door to compound tenses. This gives us six distinct forms, each expressing a different shade of time and voice.

We'll use "por hacer" as our running model across all six to show the parallel structure.

1. El Infinitivo Simple muy común

The baseline. A simple, direct action — no time framing, no voice shift. This is your default and covers the vast majority of situations.

prep + infinitivo → por hacer = "for doing"
  • Gracias por ayudar con el proyecto. Thanks for helping with the project.
  • Salí de casa sin comer. I left the house without eating.
  • Terminó la tarea antes de salir. He finished the homework before leaving.

2. El Infinitivo Compuesto común

The completed action — something that already happened. "Haber" goes in the infinitive (satisfying the preposition rule), followed by the past participle. Common in both speech and writing whenever you're looking back at a finished event.

prep + haber + participio → por haber hecho = "for having done"
  • Ella está feliz por haber ganado. She is happy for having won.
  • Me multaron por haber estacionado mal. They fined me for having parked badly.
  • Después de haber comido, salimos a caminar. After having eaten, we went for a walk.

3. El Infinitivo Progresivo menos común

Emphasizes that an action was ongoing at the relevant moment. "Estar" takes the infinitive slot, followed by the gerund. This form is real but less common in everyday speech — most of the time, the simple infinitive covers the same ground. Use this only when the "in the middle of" emphasis genuinely matters.

prep + estar + gerundio → por estar haciendo = "for being in the middle of doing"

Register note: If the simple infinitive sounds fine, use it. The progressive adds emphasis on duration or interruption, not new meaning. "Se disculpó por llegar tarde" is perfectly natural; "por estar llegando tarde" would only make sense if you're emphasizing the ongoing lateness.

  • Estoy cansado de estar esperando. I'm tired of waiting (and waiting and waiting).
  • Se disculpó por estar hablando por teléfono cuando llegué. He apologized for being on the phone when I arrived.
  • Lo echaron del cine por estar haciendo ruido. They kicked him out of the theater for making noise (ongoing disruption).

4. El Infinitivo Pasivo menos común

Used when the action is being done to the subject rather than by them. "Ser" takes the infinitive slot, followed by a past participle that agrees in gender and number with the subject. Moderately common in writing and formal speech; casual conversation tends to restructure around it.

prep + ser + participio → por ser hecho = "for being done (to someone)"

Register note: In casual speech, you'd often hear an active restructuring instead. "Está frustrado porque lo ignoran" is more natural than "por ser ignorado," though both are correct. Recognize this form when you read it; use the active version when you speak.

  • Él está frustrado por ser ignorado. He is frustrated for being ignored.
  • El perro saltó de alegría al ser rescatado. The dog jumped with joy upon being rescued.
  • Están nerviosas por ser evaluadas. They (f.) are nervous about being evaluated.

5. El Infinitivo Pasivo Compuesto formal / escrito

Combines the perfect (haber) with the passive (ser + participle). Expresses that something was done to the subject in the past. This is firmly in the territory of formal writing, journalism, and legal/academic language. In conversation, speakers almost always restructure.

prep + haber sido + participio → por haber sido hecho = "for having been done"

Register note: Recognize this when reading newspapers, legal documents, or formal essays. In speech, you'd hear the active version: "Está enojado porque lo echaron" rather than "por haber sido despedido." Both are grammatically correct; one sounds bookish, the other natural.

  • Está enojado por haber sido despedido. He is angry for having been fired.
  • El gato está molesto por haber sido bañado. The cat is annoyed for having been bathed.
  • Lloraron de emoción por haber sido elegidos. They cried with emotion for having been chosen.

6. El Infinitivo Progresivo Compuesto raro en el habla

The most complex form — combines perfect (haber) with progressive (estado + gerund). Describes an ongoing action in the past that is now finished. You'll encounter this in written narrative and formal contexts, but almost never in casual speech. If a simpler form works, use it.

prep + haber estado + gerundio → por haber estado haciendo = "for having been doing"

Register note: This is the most "textbook" of the six forms. In conversation, "Me disculpo por dormirme" or "por haberme dormido" would cover the same territory as "por haber estado durmiendo." Reserve this form for when the emphasis on prolonged past duration is important — a deliberate narrative choice, not an everyday construction.

  • Me disculpo por haber estado durmiendo. I apologize for having been sleeping.
  • Está agotada por haber estado corriendo. She is exhausted from having been running.
  • Lo castigaron por haber estado mintiendo. They punished him for having been lying.
