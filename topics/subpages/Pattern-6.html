Patrón 6

The Impersonal Pattern se General Truths

✦
Removing the actor to state general rules or universal habits

This pattern is the Spanish equivalent of saying "One does this," "People do this," or "It is said." You use this structure when the specific person performing the action is irrelevant, unknown, or applies to society at large.

①
Universal Rulesintransitive
NO DIRECT OBJECT
Se + Verb (3rd Person Singular) + [Adverb/Preposition]

When stating a general habit or rule without a direct object (nobody is "receiving" the action), the verb must always be singular.

  • Acá se vive muy bien. One lives very well here / People live well here. — A general statement of condition.
  • No se puede fumar. One cannot smoke / Smoking is not allowed. — Stating a societal rule.
②
The Passive "Se"transitive
THE OBJECT IS THE DRIVER
Se + Verb (Singular/Plural) + Noun

When an action is performed on an object, but the actor doesn't matter, Spanish elevates the object to become the grammatical subject. The verb must conjugate to match the noun being acted upon.

  • Se habla español. Spanish is spoken. — "Español" is singular, so the verb is "habla".
  • Se venden autos. Cars are sold / They sell cars. — "Autos" is plural, dictating the plural verb "venden".

¿Cuál uso?

Singular vs. Plural Conjugation
Is the statement a general rule or condition with no object following the verb?
→
Singular e.g., Se come bien.
Is an action being performed on a singular noun?
→
Singular e.g., Se alquila casa.
Is an action being performed on a plural noun?
→
Plural e.g., Se alquilan casas.
Nota · Uso Rioplatense In Argentina and Uruguay, the Impersonal "Se" is frequently combined with the intensifier re to express general societal vibes or shared experiences.

Example: "En ese bar se está re bien." (The vibe is really good in that bar / One feels really good in that bar).
Example: "Se nota re falso." (It comes across as really fake / One notices it's fake).
