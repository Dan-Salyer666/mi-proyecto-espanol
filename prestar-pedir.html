Los Verbos Principales
prestar
to lend / to loan (lender's perspective)
The most fundamental verb. The subject is always the person giving the item.
¿Me prestas tu cargador?Can you lend me your charger?
Te presto el coche este fin de semana.I'll lend you the car this weekend.
pedir prestado/a
to borrow (borrower's perspective)
The most common way to say "to borrow." Prestado must agree in gender/number with the object.
¿Puedo pedirte prestado el bolígrafo?Can I borrow your pen?
Le pedí prestada la bici a mi hermano.I borrowed my brother's bike.
tomar prestado/a
to borrow / to take as a loan
Slightly more formal or written register. Implies taking/using the item rather than making a request. Common in LatAm.
Tomé prestado el libro de la biblioteca.I borrowed the book from the library.
sacar prestado/a
to borrow / to take out on loan
Most commonly used when borrowing from institutions — libraries, banks. Implies a formal checkout.
Saqué prestados tres libros de la biblioteca.I checked out three books from the library.
dejar
to let / to lend informally
Very casual synonym for prestar. The most colloquial way to say "lend me." Common in Spain.
¿Me dejas tu teléfono un momento?Can I borrow your phone for a sec?
fiarse / fiar
to give credit / to lend on trust
Used for lending money/goods on trust, often in shops. Me fías = put it on my tab.
¿Me fías hasta el viernes?Can you spot me until Friday?
¿Me prestas? vs. Pedir prestado

¿Me prestas…? / ¿Me dejas…?

Direct request using prestar or dejar conjugated — you're asking the other person to perform the action of lending.

¿Me prestas el coche?Will you lend me the car?
¿Me dejas tu chaqueta?Can I borrow your jacket?
  • More direct, more common in speech
  • Simpler and shorter
  • Very natural in everyday conversation

¿Puedo pedir prestado…?

Indirect request framing the borrower as taking action — more polite in register, adds a layer of formality or care.

¿Puedo pedirte prestado el coche?May I borrow your car?
¿Podría pedir prestada tu chaqueta?Could I possibly borrow your jacket?
  • More polite / formal
  • Common in writing
  • Slightly more cumbersome for casual speech
Bottom line: In daily spoken Spanish, ¿me prestas…? and ¿me dejas…? are overwhelmingly more common than ¿puedo pedir prestado…?. The latter sounds more careful or formal — you'd use it with a boss, a neighbor you don't know well, or in writing. Among friends and family, ¿me lo prestas? or simply ¡déjame tu…! is the norm.
Guía de Registro

A full spectrum of how to ask, from hanging out with close friends to formal business environments.

Register Spanish English Notes
Ultra casual ¡Oye, dame tu móvil! Hey, give me your phone! Blunt. Among close friends only. Technically "give" not "lend".
Very casual ¿Me dejas el coche un momento? Can I borrow the car a sec? Everyday friend/family speech. Dejar = most casual lend.
Casual ¿Me prestas tu cargador? Can you lend me your charger? Standard casual. Prestar conjugated. No frills.
Neutral ¿Podrías prestarme tu paraguas? Could you lend me your umbrella? Conditional makes it politer. Safe for acquaintances.
Neutral ¿Me puedes prestar tu bolígrafo? Can you lend me your pen? Poder + infinitive softens slightly.
Neutral ¿Puedo pedirte prestado el libro? More careful. Works with teachers, acquaintances. Can I borrow your book?
Formal ¿Le importaría prestarme su pluma? Very polite. Usted implied. Professional setting. Would you mind lending me your pen?
Formal ¿Sería posible que me prestara…? Would it be possible for you to lend me…? Subjunctive. Written or high-register professional.
Very formal Le solicito un préstamo de… I am requesting a loan of… Financial/legal context. Préstamo = the noun "loan".
Reglas Gramaticales

Agreement Rules for prestado

When using pedir prestado, tomar prestado, or sacar prestado, the word prestado must agree in gender and number with the noun being borrowed — not with the person.

Pedí prestado el bolígrafo → prestado (masculine singular)
Pedí prestada la bici → prestada (feminine singular)
Pedí prestados los apuntes → prestados (masculine plural)
Pedí prestadas las sillas → prestadas (feminine plural)
This rule is often skipped in very casual speech (you'll hear natives say pedí prestado las llaves) but correct usage requires agreement.

Indirect Object Pronouns (IOPs)

The key to borrowing/lending sentences is the indirect object pronoun that appears before the verb. It answers "to/from whom?"

PronounMeansExampleTranslation
meto/from me¿Me prestas…?Will you lend me…?
teto/from youTe presto mi abrigo.I'll lend you my coat.
leto/from him/her/ustedLe presté el coche.I lent him/her the car.
nosto/from us¿Nos prestas la tienda?Will you lend us the tent?
osto/from you all (Spain)Os dejo las llaves.I'll lend you all the keys.
lesto/from themLes presté dinero.I lent them money.
Casos Especiales y Matices

1. Borrowing Money

Money has its own register of terms that go beyond simple prestar.

¿Me puedes prestar 20 euros?Can you lend me 20 euros? (casual)
¿Me haces un préstamo?Can you give me a loan? (neutral/semi-formal)
Solicité un crédito / préstamo al banco.I applied for a loan from the bank. (formal/financial)
¿Me fías hasta el lunes?Can you spot me until Monday? (very casual)

2. Returning What Was Borrowed

Asking for things back uses devolver (to return/give back).

¿Cuándo me devuelves el libro?When are you giving back my book?
Tengo que devolverte el dinero.I have to pay you back the money.
¿Me lo puedes regresar?Can you give it back to me? (regresar = return in LatAm)

3. Regional Variations

Spain ¿Me lo dejas?Most natural casual form in Spain.
LatAm ¿Me lo prestas?Standard natural form in Latin America.
LatAm ¿Me lo regalas?Literally "give it to me" but used loosely for lending.
Col/Ven ¿Me lo facilitas?Can you make it available to me? (polite)

4. Common Fixed Expressions

  • Prestar atención — to pay attention (NOT borrow attention)
  • Prestar servicio — to provide/render a service
  • Prestar ayuda — to offer/render help
  • Prestarse a algo — to lend oneself to something / to be suitable for
  • Estar en préstamo — to be on loan
⚠ Prestar in fixed phrases like prestar atención has nothing to do with borrowing.
